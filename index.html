Background

What is a Digital Product Passport?

A DPP is a verifiable digital record attached to a physical product — scan it, and you see what it's made of, where it came from, and its environmental footprint.

Under the EU's ESPR, the DPP becomes mandatory in the years ahead — the regulation, not a nice-to-have.

Questions

The things you're about to ask.

How can you prove something without giving me the data?

That's a zero-knowledge proof. A prover convinces a verifier that a statement is true — while revealing nothing beyond the fact that it's true.

This is what lets you prove your passport is genuine without handing over a single supplier name.

Do I have to replace my existing systems?

No. We read from what you already run — no rip-and-replace. Each step signs its own record on-site, and the encrypted data stays in your factory.

Where does my data actually live?

In your factory, encrypted. Only a proof goes on-chain — there is no shared database and no central copy of your supply graph. At no point does Kanesh, or your buyer, hold the data you don't choose to share.

What does my buyer see?

They scan and get a yes. Selective disclosure means they see compliance — not your supplier list, not your costs, not your process.

Why blockchain at all?

For immutability. Once a record is anchored, nobody can alter or backdate it — including us. That's what makes the passport something your buyer can check rather than something they have to trust you on.

When do I actually need this?

Textiles are indicated for 2027 under the ESPR rollout. The catch is that the data you'll need to prove has to be collected before then, not after — which makes the practical starting point considerably earlier than the deadline.
